Philosophy

My philosophy for my career in robotics is rooted in the belief that robotics should serve humans, not the other way around. I see robots and artificial intelligence not as human replacements, but as an empowerment of current human capabilities, so we can focus on more pressing issues. My objective is to engineer autonomous robots that are mechanically reliable, allowing them to operate alongside people.

I first explored robotics through mechanical engineering, designing an over-terrain vehicle (OTV) drive train and navigation algorithm. These projects gave me a foundation in how autonomous systems interact to produce physical outcomes. When the OTV successfully navigated an obstacle course, avoiding obstructions and adjusting its path to reach the end site, I realized how much I enjoyed engineering robotics.

Later, while learning to code through game development, I eventually experimented with computer vision to create an aimbot using OpenCV. Though I didn't fully realize it at the time, I was also teaching myself how machines interpret data and estimate spatial position. That curiosity is what first drew me to AI, and further interested me in robotics.

These experiences shape my core belief: robotics requires interdisciplinary respect and collaboration. You can not be a programmer who ignores physics, nor can you be an engineer who ignores the intelligence embedded in the machine. This means that the best robots are not defined by their complexity, but rather by how their software and hardware work in unison to solve problems. With this belief, I strive to build robots that are simple, reliable, and designed with the people they serve in mind.

Reflection

The projects in this portfolio show my curiosity and my problem-solving skills. These experiences have also strengthened my skills in programming, engineering, and understanding of how machines interact with the world. They also reflect my persistence and passion for building intelligent and reliable systems.
